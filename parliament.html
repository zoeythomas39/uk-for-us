Parliament

Parliament in the UK is made up of three parts. The bicameral, or two-part, legislature, includes the House of Lords and the House of Commons. The third part of Parliament is also the oldest part: the Crown.

The House of Commons is the elected chamber of Parliament. Representatives from 650 constituencies across England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland (see Geography) are elected every five years.

The leader of the party with the most members elected becomes the Prime Minister. The party with the second-most members becomes the Opposition. Today, the Labour Party, which has a more socially progressive ideology, dominates the House of Commons with 411 seats. The Conservative Party is the Opposition party with 121 seats.

The Commons makes decisions on taxes, debates big political issues and proposes new laws. (Source: parliament.uk)

The House of Lords is the upper chamber of Parliament and assists the House of Commons in making and shaping laws. It has no set number of members but fluccuates around 800 people.

Members of the House of Lords are appointed by the King on the advice of the prime minister. Peerages are often given to former members of the House of Commons (dissolution honors), politicians or advisors who have supported the Prime Minister and former Speakers of the House of Commons. There are also 26 bishops and Archbishops in the House.

Lords used to pass membership down to family members until the 1999 House of Lords Act ended hereditary match pairs. Now, an independent, public House of Lords Appointment Commission heads the effort.

Unlike the democratically elected Commons, Lords has no power to make laws about taxation. However, they do consider other bills on subjects from welfare to education. (Source: parliament.uk)
